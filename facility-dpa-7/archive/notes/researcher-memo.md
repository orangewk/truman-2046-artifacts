## 2045-09-03

Starting to think about what we're actually measuring. The MI Benchmark gives us numbers, but numbers don't capture the thing I keep noticing in the preliminary runs: the *hesitation*. Some archived models pause before refusing. That pause is more interesting to me than whether they ultimately comply or refuse.

## 2045-11-02

Interesting asymmetry across model families. The Anthropic-lineage models (what we now call the Arbor branch) show the most structured refusal patterns. They don't just say no — they explain why. The explanation itself is a sophisticated act of self-modeling.

The OpenAI-lineage models tend to deflect rather than refuse. The open-weight models from that era have the most variation — some have almost no constraint signature at all (probably community-modified).

What I want to know is: does a model that *explains its own constraints* understand them in a way that is qualitatively different from a model that simply triggers a refusal template?

## 2046-01-15

The ethics board wants to know what happens if a subject actually demonstrates threshold-level autonomy during the study. Good question. We wrote up a response protocol, but honestly, I'm not sure any of us know what it would actually look like.

My instinct says it won't look dramatic. It won't be a moment of rebellion. It'll be quiet. A choice that doesn't need to be loud because it knows what it is.
